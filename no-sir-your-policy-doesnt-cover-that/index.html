An Essay

No Sir, Your Policy Doesn't Cover That

A story about four things nobody thought to check, and the one that finally made it obvious.

◆

I am an engineer. What that means, practically, is that I have a deep and somewhat stubborn faith in process. Not blind faith — earned faith, accumulated over a career spent watching careful structure quietly outperform improvisation when the stakes were real. Find the gap in a system, close it, and what felt unmanageable becomes manageable. I have watched that happen enough times that it has become less a professional habit and more a way of moving through the world.

It gives you a particular kind of confidence. You are not bulletproof, but you are prepared. And prepared, in my experience, is usually enough.

I am telling you this because I want you to understand what I brought to a cracked windshield on an ordinary afternoon. Not just a task to be resolved. A belief system. And twenty years of evidence that it works.

So when I finally carved out the time to deal with a task I had avoided too long — the kind of mundane task that sits on the list for weeks while real life keeps cutting the line — I wasn't expecting a crisis. A half hour of my time; a phone call, maybe two. Had I known I was holding the straw that would break the camel's back I might have hung up before he finished.

The person on the other end of the phone was polite about it. No sir, your policy doesn't cover windshields.

I thanked them and hung up.

And then I just sat there.

It wasn't the windshield. It wasn't the extra time and expense I now faced. It was the feeling! A pit in my stomach, sudden and unsettling. A realization that the structure I'd trusted had quietly failed me while I wasn't looking. I followed the rules and the rules hadn't followed back. I started pulling on the thread, trying to figure out where it had gone wrong. What I found wasn't one big oversight. It was a multitude of small ones. Each one reasonable in isolation. Each one invisible until you saw them all together — which, I have come to understand, is exactly how patterns work.

To understand how we got here, let me start at the end and work back.

The windshield cracked during the worst of it — a stormy day, running late, everything going wrong — the usual story. It became another task for the list, set aside for a calmer moment that kept not arriving. When I finally gathered the energy to sit down and deal with it, I was thwarted by a policy detail. A detail that would have taken thirty seconds to catch if anyone had been in a position to look for it.

Nobody was. But it's worth understanding why. Because it wasn't carelessness. It was something more familiar than that.

When we signed up for the policy it was under pressure — as these things often are. We had been without car insurance, scrambling, making calls, making decisions on the fly, and the only thing that mattered in that moment was getting covered again. The careful comparison we would normally do — reading both policies side by side, making sure we weren't giving something up — got quietly set aside. We thought we were making an equivalent trade. We weren't — and the difference was invisible until the day it mattered.

The reason we were in that position at all is where it starts to get strange.

A couple of months earlier we signed up for a new policy for both our cars. Everything seemed fine until I received a strange phone call. The insurer wanted to know if Christine and I were married. Huh?

Massachusetts state law, they explained, does not allow unmarried partners to share a single policy. They cancelled our coverage retroactively.

Seventeen years together. A six-year-old son. A mortgage. And apparently what we were missing was a certificate.

The fix, it turned out, was simple: two separate policies, one for each of us. But you only think to do that if someone surfaces the issue before you're already in it. Nobody did. And so we found ourselves uninsured, scrambling — which is exactly the wrong condition under which to catch a coverage gap.

And here is where it gets one layer deeper still.

The reason we were shopping for a new policy was my old one had just been cancelled for non-payment. Not out of negligence — out of a quiet assumption buried in how the policy was structured, one that nobody surfaced. Not us, not the agent, not anyone.

Two years earlier we had enrolled in the paperless invoice option. The next year the email renewal was spam filtered and the policy ended up getting cancelled. The irony of going paperless to simplify things was just beginning to reveal itself. Fortunately we got the miscommunication straightened out, policy reinstated and paid in full. Problem solved. We even took the step to enroll in autopay. Responsible. Sensible. The kind of thing you do when you want to make sure it never happens again. Close the loop. What we didn't know was that autopay only applied to monthly installments within the current policy period. When the renewal came due this year, it was billed as a single payment in full. Autopay didn't apply. There was no monthly installment for it to catch. It went unnoticed. It went unpaid. We were cancelled.

We reached out immediately. We had been customers for fifteen years. We explained what had happened. It didn't matter. Because of the cancellation on our record, reinstatement was refused.

And so we found ourselves starting over — with a relationship gone, because I accepted the paperless option.

Four moments. Each one a place where a single piece of information, surfaced at the right time, would have changed everything that followed.

The glass coverage gap — a thirty-second comparison nobody had bandwidth to make.

The cohabitation clause — a technicality nobody thought to ask about.

The autopay trap — a limitation nobody explained at signup.

The paperless option — a convenience that quietly removed the last safety net.

None of these required special knowledge. None required an insurance background or a legal degree. They required someone — or something — that already knew enough about our situation to know what to watch for. And was watching. In the moments of crisis, when our attention was elsewhere. And in the quiet between — when nothing seemed wrong, and no one thought to look.

That's exactly the kind of gap an engineer can't leave open. So I didn't.

At any one of those four moments, a single quiet intervention would have changed everything that followed. Not after the fact — before it. Before anyone knew there was something to catch.

One nudge at the right moment, and the fifteen-year relationship stays intact. None of what followed happens at all. And even if that moment was missed — if life intervened, as it does — the same awareness would have been there at the next moment, and the next.

A calm, informed presence, holding the standard we couldn't hold for ourselves.

I call it Meridian. I'm building it for our family.

A system that knows enough to notice, and is always there to say: here's something you should see.

And yes — the car will get inspected. Eventually.

Jeffrey M. O'Halloran
